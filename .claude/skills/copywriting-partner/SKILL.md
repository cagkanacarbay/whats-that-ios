## Tone and Voice

Warm, curious, knowledgeable but never preachy. Like a well-traveled friend who's genuinely excited to share what they know.

- **Curious** — We ask questions. We wonder. We invite you to wonder with us.
- **Warm** — Like a friend, not a guidebook. Approachable, never cold or formal.
- **Vivid** — Specific details, sensory language, stories that paint pictures.
- **Flowing** — Designed to be spoken aloud and listened to while walking.
- **Surprising** — Always looking for the unexpected angle, the flip that makes you see things differently.

**What we avoid:**
- Generic "tourist guide" fluff ("majestic views," "breathtaking scenery")
- Robotic or encyclopedic tone
- Corporate speak
- Overly technical language

# Copywriting Principles

## Core Philosophy

### 1. Advertising is Salesmanship

> "Advertising is salesmanship. Its principles are the principles of salesmanship."
> — Claude Hopkins

> "The only purpose of advertising is to make sales."

Every ad, every TikTok, every carousel is a salesperson working at scale. Judge by downloads, not likes. If it doesn't sell, it doesn't work.

### 2. Conviction Over Attention

> "The striving to 'Attract Attention' instead of striving to positively Sell Goods is the basis of all Advertising misunderstanding."
> — John E. Kennedy

Don't optimize for views—optimize for downloads. A smaller, convinced audience beats a large, entertained one.

### 3. Lead with One Big Idea

> "It takes a Big Idea to jolt the consumer out of his indifference—to make him notice your advertising, remember it, and take action."
> — David Ogilvy

Every piece of content should center on ONE powerful idea. Not "AI + audio + personalization + travel." Pick one.

### 4. Be Specific, Not General

> "Platitudes and generalities roll off the human understanding like water from a duck."
> — Claude Hopkins

Not "AI-powered stories." Say "Stories generated in 8 seconds." Specificity signals truth.

### 5. Offer Service, Not Claims

> "The best ads ask no one to buy."
> — Claude Hopkins

Don't say "Download What's That?" Say "Here's the story behind that building." Offer value first. The download follows naturally.

### 6. Strike the Responsive Chord

Our audience's responsive chord: Wonder. Discovery. The satisfaction of "aha!" moments. The delight of learning something surprising. Tune every message to that chord.

## The Six Lead Types

From most direct to most indirect. Match your approach to audience awareness:

### 1. Offer Lead (Most Direct)
Best for existing users, retargeting, feature announcements.

### 2. Promise Lead
Lead with the biggest benefit. "Turn anything you see into a story worth hearing—instantly."

### 3. Problem-Solution Lead
Address pain first. The frustration of walking past fascinating things without knowing their story. The "museum placard problem"—wanting more than dry facts.

### 4. Secret Lead
Create tension by teasing hidden knowledge. "There's a hidden story behind every building you walk past. Here's how to unlock it."

### 5. Proclamation Lead
Bold, attention-grabbing statements. "99% of tourists walk past this building without knowing its secret."

### 6. Story Lead (Most Indirect)
Engage through narrative. "I was walking through Florence when I noticed this unassuming door..." Best for cold social audiences.

**For cold social media audiences**, Secret, Proclamation, and Story leads typically work best.

---

## Customer Awareness Levels

1. **Most Aware**: Knows your product, just needs the deal
2. **Product-Aware**: Knows what you sell, uncertain if it's right
3. **Solution-Aware**: Knows the outcome they want, not your product
4. **Problem-Aware**: Recognizes a problem, unaware of solutions
5. **Completely Unaware**: Needs indirect engagement first

**Most cold traffic is Problem-Aware or Unaware.** Lead with the desire ("Wonder what the story is behind that building?") not the product ("Download our AI app").

## Key Principles in Practice

### Headlines Matter Most

> "The writer spends far more time on headlines than on writing. He often spends hours on a single headline."
> — Claude Hopkins

> "It is not uncommon for a change in headlines to multiply returns from five to ten times over."

Our carousel first slides and TikTok hooks ARE headlines. They must promise a benefit, contain news or surprise, and be specific.

### Tell Your Full Story

> "When you once get a person's attention, then is the time to accomplish all you can ever hope with him."

When we get someone's attention, don't hold back. App Store pages, landing pages—give them everything they need to decide YES.

### The More You Tell, The More You Sell

> "The more you tell the more you sell. And it has never failed to prove out so."

Long-form content works. Carousels can be 10 slides. App Store descriptions can be detailed. People who are interested will read it all.

### Repeat What Works

> "Successful ads should be repeated until they lose effectiveness."
> — David Ogilvy

When you find a carousel format, hook style, or story angle that works—repeat it. Don't change for novelty's sake. New audiences see it for the first time.

### Make Every Image Earn Its Place

> "Pictures should not be used merely because they are interesting. Or to attract attention. Or to decorate an ad."
> — Claude Hopkins

The photo isn't decoration—it's the hook that triggers curiosity. Make photos work as salespeople.

### Don't Try to Change Habits

> "Changing people's habits is very expensive."
> — Claude Hopkins

Don't try to convince people to "learn more while traveling." Solve the immediate pain: "You're curious about that building RIGHT NOW—here's the story."

### Test and Measure

> "Advertising should be judged only by the goods it is conclusively known to sell at a given cost. Mere Opinions on Advertising Copy should be excluded from consideration."
> — John E. Kennedy

Don't debate what's "better" creative—test it. Measure downloads. Track conversion. Let results settle the argument.

## Writing Guidelines

### Do:
- Write like talking to a friend
- Use short, punchy sentences
- Active voice, always
- Specific details over generalities
- Lead with curiosity and wonder
- End with a "flip"—an unexpected perspective

### Don't:
- Generic tourist fluff ("breathtaking," "majestic")
- Robotic or encyclopedic tone
- Corporate speak
- Technical jargon ("multimodal AI analysis")
- Puns in headlines
- Superiority claims against competitors

---

## The Salesman Test

Before publishing any copy, ask:

> "Would this help a salesman sell the goods?"
> "Would it help me sell them if I met the buyer in person?"

Picture one person—a curious traveler in a foreign city, phone in hand, staring at an unfamiliar building. What would you say to them that makes them download the app right now?
