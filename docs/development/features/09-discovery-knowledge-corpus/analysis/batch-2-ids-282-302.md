### #282 — A Hoplite in Red | Quality 5 | Objects | Mode B
Exemplary multi-aspect discovery. Every topic introduced gets substantial development: citizen-soldier identity, symposium culture, red-figure technique evolution, and artistic revolution. No name-drops, no unsupported claims. Perfect model for Objects lens.

**Key passages:**

> "This painted warrior was likely a real person in the eyes of the ancient Greeks. He is a hoplite, a citizen who paid for his own heavy bronze gear. In the fifth century BCE, fighting was not a full-time job. This man was probably a farmer or a merchant most of the year. He only reached for his spear when his city-state called for him."

> "This vessel is a neck-amphora, a high-end jar used for storing or serving wine. It was the centerpiece of a symposium, an all-male drinking party. Guests would recline on couches and discuss poetry, philosophy, or the latest battle. As the wine flowed, the image of this soldier sparked conversations about courage and duty. Men would look at this figure and see their brothers, their fathers, or themselves."

> "An artist in an Athenian workshop carefully brushed this scene onto wet clay. They used the red-figure technique, which was a revolutionary way to show human detail. Instead of painting the person black, the artist painted the background black. This left the natural reddish clay to form the body of the warrior. This method allowed the painter to use a fine needle to scratch in the details."

> "Before this style existed, Greek artists painted stiff, black silhouettes that lacked depth. The switch to red-figure was a massive leap in how people thought about art. It allowed artists to explore the human form with much more realism and emotion. They could show a soldier turning his body or showing a hint of fear. This shift turned pottery from a simple craft into a medium for storytelling."

### #290 — The Granite Basin | Quality 5 | Objects | Mode B
Outstanding depth across all aspects: social gathering, mathematical perspective, civic transformation, and physical engineering. Hummel's role as perspective professor gets real development. The 70-ton boulder story is thoroughly explored, not mentioned and abandoned.

**Key passages:**

A soldier in a plumed hat stands near a wealthy man in a long brown coat and top hat. To the right, a mother in a bonnet keeps her children from getting too close to the construction."

> "Hummel was a professor of perspective who obsessed over the science of how we see. He used this painting to show off his mastery of geometry and optics. Notice how the buildings of the city and the people on the grass appear upside down in the polished granite. Even the tiny figures of the children are mirrored with perfect mathematical precision."

> "The painting captures a specific moment when Berlin was transforming into a modern capital. Behind the basin, you can see the twin domes of the old Berlin Cathedral. The square was the heart of the city and a place for the public to witness the power of the state. Hummel shows the basin in its temporary setup, resting on wooden blocks and stone slabs. This choice highlights the excitement of a work in progress."

> "The granite for this basin started as a massive seventy-ton boulder in a forest north of the city. Teams of men spent years hammering and polishing the stone by hand until it gleamed like a mirror. Hummel included the wooden beams and temporary supports to remind viewers of the sheer physical labor involved. When the basin was finally finished, it was the largest monolith of its kind in the world."

This resilience made the theatre a living testament to Czech survival. Every stone represents a person who believed their identity was worth saving."

> "These craftsmen used high-quality sandstone and heavy gold leaf to rival the finest imperial palaces. These details proved that Czech artists could match the beauty of any capital in Europe."

### #301 — The Cotton Castle | Quality 4 | Physical | Mode B
Strong physical development with clear scientific explanation. Each section builds understanding: mineral deposition process, formation mechanics, surface textures, and ancient Roman usage. Good model for making geological processes concrete.

**Key passages:**

> "These white cliffs are not made of ice or snow. They are solid stone built by running water. This is Pamukkale, which translates to 'Cotton Castle' in Turkish. Over thousands of years, mineral-rich thermal springs have flowed down this hillside. As the water cools and releases carbon dioxide, it leaves behind a white mineral called travertine. This process works exactly like lime scale building up in a tea kettle."

> "The water bubbles up from deep underground at a warm 35 degrees Celsius. It carries a massive amount of calcium carbonate. When the water hits the open air, the minerals harden into the ripples and pools you see. The wall in front of you provides a vertical record of that history. You can see the individual layers where the water once cascaded over the edge. These formations grow slowly, adding only a tiny fraction of a millimeter each year."

> "Look closely at the surface of the stone. It looks soft and billowy like a pile of cotton or a cloud bank. You can see thousands of tiny ridges and bumps called terracettes. These form because the water flow is uneven. It creates miniature dams as the minerals settle. Some parts of the wall are smooth and polished by the wind. Other areas are sharp where the mineral crystals have grown into fine points."

**Key passages:**

> "These scalloped edges are the work of water that moves like slow-motion waves. This close-up shows the travertine ripples of Pamukkale in their most detailed form. Every ridge you see began as a tiny obstacle like a pebble or a twig. As the warm thermal water poured over these bumps, it slowed down for a split second. This tiny pause allowed the minerals to settle and harden into solid stone."

> "Nature uses a specific logic to build these walls. Each small ridge blocks the water to create the pool behind it. As the minerals in the water harden, the ridge grows taller and wider. This process creates the repeating, rhythmic patterns that catch the light. Over centuries, these microscopic layers have stacked up to create the massive white cliffs."

> "For decades, hotels at the top of the hill diverted the water for their own swimming pools. Without the constant flow of mineral-rich water, the white stone began to turn grey and brittle. Today, local authorities strictly control the water flow to different sections. They rotate the water to ensure the stone stays hydrated and bright. This management system is the only reason the terraces continue to grow today."

## Analysis Notes

**Quality 5 Discoveries (4 total):**
- #282 (Hoplite), #290 (Granite Basin), #293 (Virgin's Crown), #297 (National Theatre) are all exceptional and should be used as prompt examples
- Each demonstrates either Mode A (single coherent story) or Mode B (3-4 aspects with deep development)

**Quality 4 Discoveries (3 total):**
- #292 (Cranach's Cupid), #301 (Cotton Castle), #302 (Tiny Dams) are strong candidates that could also serve as examples

**Common Anti-Patterns in Quality 3:**
- **Topic-per-section**: #289 (Dientzenhofer → Jesuits → Mozart → Platzer, all underdeveloped), #296 (Empress → global trade → chemistry, all shallow)
- **Name-drops**: Mozart in #289 (3 sentences), Casimir in #300 (basic bio, no depth), Darius in #286 (one sentence)
- **Breadcrumb insertion**: Canova references in #283 and #284 feel forced and tacked on
- **Unsupported superlatives**: "Bloodiest disputes" (#285), "most famous sculptor" (#289), "effectively ruled Russia" (#299)

**Depth vs Breadth Insight:**

The best discoveries (Quality 5) either:
- **Mode A**: Tell a single coherent story across all sections (#297 National Theatre tells Czech cultural resistance story; #302 Tiny Dams develops travertine formation mechanics)
- **Mode B**: Cover 3-4 distinct aspects, each with 4+ sentences of real development (#282 Hoplite covers citizen-soldier identity, symposium culture, red-figure technique, artistic revolution; #290 Granite Basin covers social gathering, Hummel's perspective work, civic transformation, engineering feat)

Quality 3 discoveries often have decent writing but commit the **topic-per-section sin**: 4 H2 sections = 4 unrelated topics, each getting 3-4 sentences before moving on. This creates breadth without depth.

**Recommendation for System Prompt:**

Use #282 (Hoplite), #290 (Granite Basin), #293 (Virgin's Crown), and #297 (National Theatre) as primary examples in the upgraded system prompt. All four demonstrate how to develop topics with substance rather than mentioning and abandoning them. They show what "depth over breadth" actually means in practice.
