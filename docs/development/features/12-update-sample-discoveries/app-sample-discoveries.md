## The Picks

### 1. The Last Senate (dev ID 265) — Venice, Italy
**Opening:** "In 1797, a government that lasted eleven centuries vanished in a single afternoon."

14 words. Eleven centuries versus one afternoon. Napoleon at the lagoon's edge, the Great Council voting to destroy itself, scarlet-robed senators walking down the same staircase where doges were crowned, the giant statues of Mars and Neptune watching them become the last men to hold power.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a painting of men in robes. App reveals you're watching a government commit suicide.

---

### 2. House of the Black Heads (dev ID 322) — Riga, Latvia
**Opening:** "Young, unmarried merchants formed an exclusive brotherhood named after a dark-skinned African saint."

Expelled the moment you married. Bombed to rubble in 1941, rebuilt stone by stone from pre-war photographs. Bachelor status was a hard membership requirement. They controlled the flow of wealth into Riga's port and guarded the city walls during war.

**The gap:** Tourist sees an ornate guild hall. App reveals the world's most exclusive bachelor club.

---

### 3. The Arm of a Saint (dev ID 309) — Vilnius, Lithuania
**Opening:** "A piece of human bone rests inside this silver hand."

A 16th-century reliquary containing a fragment of Saint Casimir, patron of Lithuania. When war or plague struck, priests carried this arm through the streets. Master goldsmiths shaped the metal to look like a hand frozen in a gesture of blessing. A crystal window reveals the actual bone inside.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a silver hand in a display case. App reveals there's a real bone inside and it was paraded through the streets to ward off plague.

---

### 4. The Doge's Giant Ego (dev ID 174) — Venice, Italy
**Opening:** "Giovanni Pesaro spent a fortune to ensure no one would ever forget his name. He was one of the wealthiest men in Venice but he was not well liked."

12,000 gold ducats from beyond the grave. Chose the spot next to the entrance so you can't avoid him. Served as Doge for only one year. Four massive figures carved from dark black marble struggle under the weight of the structure. Two bronze skeletons hide in the niches holding hourglasses — even with all his gold, Pesaro couldn't escape the same end as everyone else.

**The gap:** Tourist sees an enormous tomb in a church. App reveals a dead man's ego project funded from his will, designed to force everyone entering to look up at him.

---

### 5. The Merchant's Moral Map (dev ID 134) — Antwerp, Belgium
**Opening:** "Look at the large scales hanging near the top of the image. On one side you see the word for debtor. On the other side is the word for creditor."

A giant woodcut print made from several blocks joined together — a 16th-century dashboard for a merchant's office. Contains a calendar, coats of arms, moral advice, and a panoramic view of the busiest port in the world. Every face and barrel tells a story of the material world.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a complex old print. App reveals it was the moral compass and business dashboard for the richest merchants on earth.

---

### 6. The Wood Inlays of Frari (dev ID 307) — Venice, Italy
**Opening:** "Marco Cozzi used zero paint to create these intricate cityscapes in 1468."

Seven years fitting thousands of tiny wood fragments together like a puzzle. Dark walnut for shadows, pale willow for sunlight. A technique called intarsia that was cutting-edge in the 1400s. Franciscan monks sat in these stalls seven times every day, starting at two in the morning.

**The gap:** Tourist sees wooden choir stalls. App reveals zero paint was used — every color comes from a different species of tree.

---

### 7. The Winged Hussars (dev ID 183) — Warsaw, Poland
**Opening:** "For over a century, these riders were considered the most dangerous men on a European battlefield."

The elite cavalry of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. Leopard skins over steel breastplates. The famous feathered wings created a terrifying sound — eagle feathers whistling, wooden frames rattling against metal, making it sound like a much larger force was attacking. Each hussar provided his own horses and equipment at enormous personal cost.

**The gap:** Tourist sees armor in a museum. App reveals the terrifying sound design built into the wings.

---

### 8. Inferno in the Palace (dev ID 177) — Venice, Italy
**Opening:** "This nightmare vision of the afterlife hung in the most secretive room in Venice. Members of the Council of Ten stared at these flames while deciding who lived and died."

Secret tribunal. Painting of hell on the wall. Judges looking at demons while sentencing people for treason. The three leaders changed every month to prevent corruption. A landscape of mechanical monsters, a hollowed-out egg serving as a tavern for the damned, a dying sun casting sickly light over crumbling towers.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a Bosch-style painting. App reveals the judges who sentenced people to death stared at it while deliberating.

---

### 9. Ghetto Heroes Monument (dev ID 316) — Warsaw, Poland
**Opening:** "The stone used to build this monument was originally ordered by Adolf Hitler for a victory arch."

The strongest first sentence in the database. No preamble, no context-setting, just a fact so ironic it stops you mid-scroll. Swedish stone, 1942, Berlin, expected victory — every sentence adds a physical, verifiable detail. Armed fighters on the front, silent victims led to death on the back.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a war memorial. App reveals the stone itself was Hitler's.

---

### 10. Accidental Sticky Notes (dev ID 219) — USA
**Opening:** "In 1968, a scientist named Spencer Silver was trying to invent a super-strong adhesive for aircraft. He failed completely."

The failed glue sat useless for five years. A choir singer named Art Fry kept losing his hymnal bookmarks and remembered the weak adhesive. The iconic yellow color only happened because the lab next door had nothing but yellow scrap paper. Every detail in the origin is accidental — the glue, the use case, the color.

**Why this matters for the app:** This proves the app works on *anything*. Not a cathedral, not a ruin, not a monument — a pad of sticky notes on your desk.

**The gap:** User sees a sticky note. App reveals a failed aircraft glue, a frustrated choir singer, and yellow scrap paper from the wrong lab.

---

### 11. Trakai's Diverse Streets (dev ID 229) — Trakai, Lithuania
**Opening:** "In 1397, Grand Duke Vytautas returned from the Black Sea with nearly four hundred families."

Year, number, origin, purpose — all in 4 sentences. Houses with exactly three windows: one for God, one for the Grand Duke, one for the family. Six centuries later, their descendants still live on the same peninsula. Mosque and church on the same street.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a pretty lakeside town. App reveals 600-year-old Tatar bodyguard families and what each window means.

---

### 12. Sobieski at Vienna (prod ID 1771) — Warsaw, Poland
**Opening:** "This painting captures the exact moment the map of Europe was rewritten."

The Battle of Vienna, 1683. King Jan III Sobieski led the largest cavalry charge in history to break the Ottoman siege. Camels in the dust, silk tents of the Grand Vizier, the white crescent moon on red flags. His letter to the Pope: "We came, we saw, and God conquered."

**The gap:** Tourist sees a battle painting. App reveals the largest cavalry charge in history and the letter that followed.

---

### 13. Polyxena Sarcophagus (dev ID 40) — Çanakkale, Turkey
**Opening:** "This massive marble box is the Polyxena Sarcophagus. It was carved around 500 BC."

Trojan myth used to honor a real death. The sacrifice of Polyxena — youngest daughter of King Priam — carved on the side. A jagged hole in the lid from ancient grave robbers. Art between Persian rule and Greek influence. A private burial turned into a lasting political statement.

**The gap:** Tourist sees an ancient stone box. App reveals it tells the story of a princess sacrificed to a ghost, and robbers smashed through the lid centuries ago.

---

### 14. Palace of Culture (dev ID 237) — Warsaw, Poland
**Opening:** "Joseph Stalin sent forty million bricks and five thousand Soviet workers to build this skyscraper in 1955."

A "gift" from the Soviet Union. Some politicians wanted to tear it down after communism fell. The Rolling Stones played a legendary concert inside. Peregrine falcons nest near the spire. Four clock faces, each over six meters wide — the highest in Europe.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a giant Soviet building. App reveals Stalin's "gift," the Stones concert, and falcon colonies at the top.

---

### 15. The Price of Fire (dev ID 125) — Berlin, Germany
**Opening:** "You are looking at the Greek titan Prometheus."

Prometheus stole fire from the gods — the spark of technology, science, and independent thought. Carved from a single massive block of Carrara marble in Rome. The eagle pecking at his liver, the chains on his wrists, the vulnerable humans at his feet. A favorite of 19th-century German thinkers obsessed with the heroic individual.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a marble sculpture. App reveals it's the patron saint of intellectual rebellion, carved from a single block over a decade.

---

### 16. A Map to the Afterlife (dev ID 57) — Berlin, Germany
**Opening:** "You are looking at sections of an ancient Egyptian Book of the Dead."

A survival manual for the dead. Forty-two denials of sin to recite perfectly. Black ink from soot, red ink for critical instructions. King Frederick William IV funded the expedition that brought these scrolls to Berlin. Three thousand years old and the carbon ink is still clear.

**The gap:** Tourist sees faded papyrus. App reveals it's a legal defense for the soul, funded by a king who wanted to rival London and Paris.

---

### 17. Ornate Wheellock (dev ID 261) — Warsaw, Poland
**Opening:** "These ornate firearms were designed to demonstrate political power through expensive technology."

A 16th-century status symbol — like owning a sports car today. The wheellock mechanism worked exactly like a modern cigarette lighter. Bone and ivory inlays transformed a weapon into art. A nobleman could carry it loaded in a holster, changing the social hierarchy of the battlefield.

**The gap:** Tourist sees an old gun. App reveals it was a diplomatic statement, a mechanical revolution, and a gentleman's accessory.

---

### 18. The Rebel Monk Methodius (dev ID 173) — Prague, Czech Republic
**Opening:** "Methodius spent two years in a dark dungeon for preaching in a language people actually understood."

The absurdity of the crime is immediately gripping. Two Greek brothers invented an entirely new alphabet from scratch. Millions of Slavic people got the ability to write their own history. Explains something (Cyrillic script) that billions of people use today.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a mosaic of a monk. App tells them this man invented an alphabet and went to prison for it.

---

### 19. Rundāle Palace (dev ID 298) — Rundāle, Latvia
**Opening:** "One man's obsession with Russian power turned this remote Latvian field into a miniature St. Petersburg."

Designed by the same architect who built the Winter Palace. The Duke was the lover of Empress Anna Ioannovna. He enjoyed the luxury for only a few years before exile to Siberia for twenty years. The palace sat empty and silent until he was allowed to return.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a yellow Baroque palace. App reveals its owner was exiled to Siberia and the halls sat empty for two decades.

---

### 20. Stoves of Rundāle (dev ID 199) — Rundāle, Latvia
**Opening:** "Nearly eighty hand-painted ceramic tiles cover this massive heating tower from top to bottom."

Dutch Delftware-style tiles on a giant heat battery. Wood burned inside a brick core for hours; the ceramic slowly released warmth long after the fire died. Servants stoked fires from hidden corridors behind the walls so royalty never saw the mess. The heat felt like an invisible miracle.

**The gap:** Tourist sees a decorative tiled column. App reveals it's a thermal battery with hidden servant passages behind the walls.
